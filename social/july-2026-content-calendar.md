**Hook:**
> CPS 230 goes live in July 2025. Most large institutions are still not compliant — and most of them know it.

**Body:**
APRA's CPS 230 Operational Risk Management standard has been in effect since 1 July 2025.

It requires APRA-regulated entities to demonstrate:
- Identification and management of material operational risks
- Documented business continuity plans
- Governance over critical third-party dependencies
- Evidence of process controls — not just policies, but working controls

Here's what we're seeing in the field:

Most institutions have the policies written. Many have governance frameworks on paper. Very few have the underlying process documentation to back them up under scrutiny.

The gap isn't intent. It's execution.

The typical finding: senior leaders can articulate their risk appetite. Middle managers can describe their controls. But the actual process — how a claim gets processed, how a payment gets approved, how an exception gets escalated — hasn't been mapped at the operational level.

CPS 230 doesn't care about your framework document. It cares whether the person doing the work can demonstrate the control in the process.

That's the gap we close.

> Power BI isn't your reporting problem. Your process is.

**Body:**
I talk to a lot of heads of analytics and operations leaders who've invested heavily in Power BI or Microsoft Fabric — and are still frustrated with their reporting.

The dashboards look good. The numbers are there. But no one trusts them.

Usually, the problem isn't Power BI. It's the process feeding it.

When data flows through undocumented manual steps, workarounds, and informal handoffs, your reporting inherits all of that noise. A slick Fabric dashboard built on a broken process is just an expensive way to visualise a problem you haven't fixed.

The sequence that works:

1. Map the process that produces the data (not just the data model)
2. Identify the control points where errors can enter
3. Document the transformation steps between source and report
4. Build the Fabric pipeline to automate the clean steps; surface the manual ones for control
5. Then build your Power BI layer on a process you understand

This sounds obvious. It almost never happens in practice.

The organisations with reporting they trust aren't the ones with the best DAX skills. They're the ones who mapped their process before they built their pipeline.

> The most dangerous process in your organisation is the one everyone says "works fine."

**Body:**
Every organisation has them.

The process that's been running "the same way for years." The one where the team lead is the only person who actually knows how it works. The one where "we've never had a problem" is the only documentation.

These are your highest-risk processes. Not because they're broken — but because you don't know if they're broken.

There's a concept in operational risk called the "grey zone" — processes that haven't failed visibly but haven't been tested under stress either. They work when the right person is in the seat, when volumes are normal, when systems behave. They fail when one of those conditions changes.

We've seen it happen. A key process owner leaves. A system upgrade changes a data format. A volume spike hits during a period when cover is thin. And suddenly the process that "worked fine" becomes the source of a material operational incident.

The fix is uncomfortable: you have to document and test the processes that feel the most stable. Not the broken ones — you already know about those.

What process in your organisation would fail silently if your best person was out for two weeks?

### Post 7 — Monday 21 July 2026
**Pillar:** `COMPLIANCE`  
**Score target:** 79+

**Hook:**
> ISO 9001 certification without process maps is just paperwork. Here's what auditors actually look for.

**Body:**
I've been involved in a number of ISO 9001 preparation engagements over the past few years. Here's what I consistently see:

Organisations spend months on their Quality Management System documentation — policies, procedures, objectives, management reviews. They get to the certification audit feeling prepared.

Then the auditor asks a process-level question.

"Walk me through how a customer complaint is handled from receipt to closure."

And instead of a clean, documented process with a traceable audit trail, they get three different answers from three different people.

ISO 9001:2015 (the current version) is explicitly process-based. Clause 4.4 requires you to determine your processes, their sequence, their interaction, and their criteria for effective operation. It's not enough to have a procedure — you need evidence of how the process actually operates.

What auditors look for:
- Process maps that reflect what actually happens (not an idealised version)
- Defined inputs, outputs, owners, and controls for each process
- Evidence of monitoring and measurement
- Records that demonstrate the process ran as documented

The organisations that pass their ISO 9001 audits on the first attempt are the ones whose documented processes match their actual practice. Closing that gap is exactly what structured process mapping achieves.

Current state when we arrived:
- 6 analysts pulling data from 4 separate systems
- Manual consolidation into a 200-row Excel model
- 3 rounds of QA, usually finding errors introduced in step 2
- 180 person-hours per month, every month

The brief: "We want this faster and more reliable."

What we actually found: the problem wasn't the reporting — it was the 12 manual data transformation steps happening before the data ever touched Excel.

Our approach:

**Week 1-2:** Mapped the current-state process. Identified every manual step, every system handoff, every transformation.

**Week 3-4:** Classified each step: automate (rules-based, high volume), simplify (rules-based, low volume), or retain (judgment-required).

**Week 5-8:** Built the Microsoft Fabric pipeline. Automated data extraction from all 4 source systems, standardised transformations, built in data quality checks at every join point.

**Week 9-10:** Built the Power BI reporting layer. Connected directly to the Fabric lakehouse. Added automated distribution on the 2nd business day of each month.

Result: 20 person-hours per month. Same report. Better data quality. Zero manual QA cycles.

ROI in month 1: the 160 hours freed up were redirected to higher-value analysis. The report went from "usually ready by day 8" to "delivered on day 2, every time."

This is passive reporting. It's better than nothing. It's not as good as it could be.

Microsoft Fabric's Data Activator (now generally available) flips this model. Instead of waiting for a human to notice a problem in a dashboard, Data Activator monitors your data continuously and triggers actions when conditions are met.

Practical examples for operations leaders:

- A KPI drops below threshold → automatically email the process owner, no one has to check a dashboard
- A transaction volume spike exceeds 150% of rolling average → create a Teams notification for the ops team
- A data load hasn't arrived by the expected time → alert the pipeline owner before the downstream report is affected
- A compliance metric moves into the amber zone → trigger a workflow in Power Automate to initiate a review task

For organisations with CPS 230 obligations, this is particularly relevant. Active monitoring of operational metrics isn't just operationally useful — it's evidence of a control operating in real time.

Building Data Activator triggers requires: Fabric capacity (F64+ recommended for enterprise), your data in OneLake, and defined thresholds per metric.

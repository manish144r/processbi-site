## 3. Banner Image Design Brief

**Dimensions:** 1128×191px (or 2256×382px @2x for Retina displays)

**Purpose:** The banner is visible when anyone visits the company page. It should communicate specialisation at a glance — no text-heavy paragraphs, no stock photo of a handshake.

**Design direction:**

- **Background:** Dark navy or charcoal (#1B2A4A or similar deep blue-grey) — professional, serious, not corporate-cheesy
- **Visual element (option A — clean/technical):** A partial, stylised swim lane diagram or process flow in a light blue or cyan accent colour — rendered as thin lines, not as a real diagram. Abstract enough to be design-forward, specific enough to communicate "process"
- **Visual element (option B — data/BI):** A stylised Power BI-style bar chart or KPI dashboard in the same accent colour — suggests the analytics output
- **Text overlay (if any):** Maximum two lines. Options:
  - Line 1: "ProcessBI" (your logo font)
  - Line 2: "Process Intelligence | Microsoft Fabric | Power BI"
  - OR: No text overlay — let the logo (lower left of profile) carry the name
- **Accent colour:** Microsoft Fabric yellow-orange (#F2C811) or a deep teal (#0E7C86) — either ties to the Microsoft ecosystem or signals analytical precision
- **Do not include:** Stock photos, team photos (there is no "team" to show), generic tech imagery (circuit boards, gears), clip art

**File format:** PNG or JPG, under 8MB

**Tools to use:** Canva (free tier), Figma (free tier), or Adobe Express. If using Canva, search for "LinkedIn Company Page Banner" template and customise.

**POST 7 — Technical how-to (beginner-friendly)**
*Day 10, 8:00 AM AEST*

```
The difference between a process map and a swim lane diagram — and when to use each:

A process map answers: what happens?
A swim lane diagram answers: who does what, and when?

Both are useful. They serve different audiences.

Use a process map when:
• You're documenting a process for the first time and need to understand the sequence
• The audience is technical (they want to see the logic, not the ownership)
• You're preparing for process simulation

Use a swim lane diagram when:
• You need to identify where handoffs occur (where work passes from one team to another)
• You're doing a process improvement review and need to show where delays or errors happen
• You're preparing documentation for an audit or compliance review
• You're briefing a new CIO/COO who needs to understand the organisation at a glance

At ProcessBI, we default to swim lanes for client-facing work. They surface accountability problems immediately — the diagram shows exactly where the ball gets dropped.

What format does your team use for process documentation?

```
Microsoft Fabric is changing what's possible for Australian enterprise BI — but there's a trap.

Fabric collapses the traditional data stack (ingest → store → transform → serve) into a single SaaS platform. For organisations already on Azure and M365, this is genuinely transformative. One lakehouse. One semantic model. DirectLake. It's faster and cheaper than a traditional Synapse + Power BI stack.

Here's the trap:

Fabric is excellent at making it fast to move data from A to B to a dashboard. That speed hides the fact that nobody has agreed on what A is, what it means, and whether the process that produces it is under control.

We've started including a "Fabric readiness assessment" in our discovery phase for new clients — a 2-day exercise that maps the data sources to the business processes that produce them, before a single Lakehouse table is created.

The organisations that skip this step end up with beautiful, fast, technically excellent dashboards that measure the wrong things.

### Hashtag Usage Rules for Posts

- Maximum 5 hashtags per post (more than 5 looks spammy; LinkedIn's algorithm does not reward hashtag stuffing)
- Always include at minimum: one service hashtag (#PowerBI or #MicrosoftFabric or #BusinessProcess) and one audience hashtag (#ManagementConsulting or #DigitalTransformation or #OperationalExcellence)
- Avoid all-caps hashtags except well-known acronyms (#BPMN, #APRA, #APS, #ERP)
- Put hashtags at the end of the post — not embedded in the text where they break reading flow
- Rotate hashtags across posts; using the same 5 on every post signals spam to the algorithm

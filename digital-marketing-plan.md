## 1. Market Positioning

### Positioning Statement

> **ProcessBI is Australia's process-first business intelligence consultancy — we diagnose how your organisation actually works, then build the Microsoft Fabric and Power BI systems that make that intelligence visible and actionable.**

This one sentence does three things: (1) anchors in process before technology, (2) names the tech stack explicitly so it appears in searches, (3) implies the gap between how organisations *think* they work and how they *actually* work — a pain every CIO, COO, and CFO recognises.

---

### 3 Differentiators vs Large Consulting Firms (Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, Accenture)

| # | Their weakness | ProcessBI advantage |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Delivery via graduate analyst teams** billed at partner rates, high coordinator overhead | **Principal-led delivery** — the person you sell to is the person who does the work. No dilution through sub-junior staff |
| 2 | **Methodology over outcomes** — frameworks sold as products, engagement length maximised | **Fixed-scope, time-boxed engagements** with clear deliverables and an explicit exit plan. Clients own the outputs from day one |
| 3 | **Generalist to a fault** — process consultants don't know Power BI, BI consultants don't know process | **Integrated capability**: process mapping → automation → Fabric/Power BI is a single practice, not three separate teams with handoff risk |

---

### 3 Differentiators vs Boutique IT Firms

| # | Their weakness | ProcessBI advantage |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Tool-first thinking** — implement the software without understanding the business process it's meant to serve | **Process diagnosis before any tool recommendation** — we map the current state, identify failure modes, then decide what to automate or measure |
| 2 | **Narrow scope** — a Power BI shop that won't touch the upstream process, or a Lean consultant who can't build a dashboard | **End-to-end**: process design → data modelling → reporting layer, handled in a single engagement |
| 3 | **No government/APRA-regulated experience** — compliance requirements, procurement rules, and audit trails are an afterthought | **Sector-specific capability** in federal and state government, financial services, and regulated utilities — we understand APS procurement, APRA CPG 235, and FOI implications |

### Channel A — LinkedIn (Primary Channel)

**Objective:** Build visible domain authority so inbound inquiries arrive pre-qualified. LinkedIn is the only channel where decision-makers in Australian enterprise and government self-segment by job title and actively engage with thought leadership.

**Company Page Strategy**

The company page is a credibility anchor, not the content engine. Decision-makers will check it after seeing a post or receiving a connection request. It must:
- Convey specialist positioning immediately (first 150 characters of About are visible without clicking "see more")
- List every service as a keyword (Specialties field is SEO-indexed)
- Show consistent posting activity (LinkedIn algorithm deprioritises dormant pages)

Content on the company page should mirror — not duplicate — personal posts. Repost personal articles to the company page. Publish 2–3 company-native posts per week separate from personal reposts.

**Content Pillars**

| Pillar | Purpose | Format | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Process Horror Stories** (anonymised) | Pattern interrupt — every enterprise reader has lived a version of this | Short post, 150–250 words, story arc: situation → breakdown → resolution | 1×/week |
| **2. Technical How-To** | Authority signal for the CIO/CDO audience; gets saved and shared by practitioners | Carousel (PDF slides, 6–10 pages) or long-form post with structure | 1×/week |
| **3. Industry Insight** | Positions ProcessBI as informed about the sector, not just the tool | Comment on a news story or regulatory development with a process/BI angle | 2×/week (lighter format) |
| **4. Behind-the-Methodology** | Differentiation — shows the ProcessBI approach vs generic consulting | Short video (60–90 sec talking head) or annotated swim lane image | 2×/month |

**Post Frequency and Format**

- Minimum: 5 posts/week across personal profile + company page combined
- Personal profile: 3–4 posts/week (algorithm rewards personal > company pages)
- Company page: 2–3 posts/week
- Optimal post time: Tuesday–Thursday, 7:30–9:00 AM AEST
- Format hierarchy by reach: video > carousel/document > image + text > text only
- Always include a question or CTA at the end of every post to drive comment engagement
- Keep line breaks aggressive — no paragraph blocks. LinkedIn truncates at ~210 characters; the first 3 lines must hook

**Connection and Follower Growth Tactics**

Week 1–4 (Foundation):
- Connect with 15–20 targeted profiles per day (LinkedIn limit: 100 connection requests/week)
- Target criteria: CIO/CDO/COO/Head of Operations in AU, 500+ employee companies, Microsoft-stack evident from their profile
- Personalise every request with one of the outreach sequences in the Outreach Playbook
- Join and engage in: Australian CIO Forum, Microsoft Fabric Community AU, APS Digital Network, Financial Services Technology AU

Week 5–12 (Momentum):
- Comment on 5–10 posts per day from target accounts and influencers (this is the fastest growth tactic — thoughtful comments on high-engagement posts surface you to new audiences)
- Target for comments: CAANZ, AIIA, ACS Digital, Gartner AU, Microsoft AU — their posts reach your ICP
- Engage on every comment your own posts receive within 4 hours (drives algorithmic recirculation)

**LinkedIn Article vs Post vs Newsletter**

| Format | Use when | Distribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Post** | Time-sensitive insight, quick thought, engagement play | Shown to connections + followers in feed |
| **Article** | Deep technical content (>800 words), evergreen reference material, case study | Indexed by Google; shared link has better click-through than external blog links |
| **Newsletter** | Regular cadence content (monthly digest, sector roundup) | Sent to subscribers + appears in feed; builds a distinct subscriber list |

Recommendation: Publish 1 Article per month on a technical topic (e.g., "How to model a process hierarchy in Microsoft Fabric"). Start a Newsletter only once you have 500+ followers — before that, the subscriber count looks thin.

**Using PRISM Social for Automated-but-Human Posting**

PRISM Social (or equivalent scheduling tool — Buffer, Hootsuite, Publer) should be used for:
- Scheduling posts 1–2 weeks in advance so publishing doesn't require daily attention
- Recycling evergreen content on a 90-day rotation (posts older than 90 days can be re-published with minor edits)
- Posting to both personal profile and company page simultaneously
- Tracking engagement metrics by post type/pillar to double down on what works

**Automation rules:**
- Never automate comments or direct messages — these will get the account flagged and destroy trust
- Draft posts in advance using the NightFactory AI pipeline; human review and approval before any scheduling
- Build a 3-week buffer of scheduled posts so a busy week doesn't create a gap

**Engagement Tactics**

Comment strategy: Post a comment within the first 30 minutes a post goes live (before the algorithm decides whether to amplify). Your comment shows up in the feeds of your connections when you engage.

Poll ideas (polls get 2–5× more impressions than standard posts):
- "When did your organisation last update its process documentation?" (Never / Over 2 years ago / In the last year / We don't have any)
- "What's the #1 barrier to useful Power BI adoption in your organisation?" (Data quality / Lack of training / Wrong metrics built / Governance issues)
- "How many weeks does a typical BI project take at your organisation to produce a first useful dashboard?"
- "Does your ERP implementation include dedicated process redesign work — or is it assumed the old processes will just map across?"

**Schema Markup for Rich Results**

Add the following schema types to processbi.com.au:

- `Organization` schema: name, url, logo, contactPoint, sameAs (LinkedIn URL)

- `BreadcrumbList` schema: on all interior pages

Use Google's Rich Results Test (search.google.com/test/rich-results) to validate after implementation.

### Channel D — Email / Nurture

**Objective:** Convert website visitors and LinkedIn followers into warm contacts who receive ongoing value before they have a buying need.

**Lead Magnet**

Offer: **"The ProcessBI Diagnostic Checklist — 47 Questions to Assess Your Organisation's Process Maturity"** (PDF, 3–4 pages)

Why this works:
- Self-selects for organisations that know they have a process problem
- The checklist itself surfaces pain — by question 10, the reader knows they need help
- Evergreen — relevant regardless of industry or maturity stage
- Easy to promote via LinkedIn post ("DM me 'checklist' and I'll send it")

Capture mechanism: Simple form on the website — first name + work email only. No friction.

**5-Email Nurture Sequence (new subscribers)**

*Email 1 — Immediate (deliver the lead magnet)*
Subject: Your ProcessBI Diagnostic Checklist is here
Body: 3 sentences. Attach checklist. Tell them what to expect from the sequence.

*Email 2 — Day 3*
Subject: The most common mistake in process improvement projects
Body: 200 words. One insight from your practice. No pitch. CTA: "Reply and tell me which section of the checklist was most confronting."

*Email 3 — Day 7*
Subject: Case in point — [anonymised scenario relevant to their likely industry]
Body: 300 words. Tell the story of a similar organisation's before/after. Soft CTA: "If your organisation is facing something similar, I'd be glad to do a no-obligation 20-minute diagnostic call."

*Email 4 — Day 14*
Subject: Why your Power BI dashboards aren't being used (it's not what you think)
Body: 250 words. Insight-led. Challenges the assumption that BI adoption is a training problem, not a process problem. CTA: Link to the most relevant blog post on processbi.com.au.

*Email 5 — Day 28*
Subject: Quick question before I stop interrupting your inbox
Body: 100 words. "I've sent you a few thoughts over the past month. If any of it was useful, I'd love to hear what challenge you're working on — reply any time. If you'd prefer not to hear from me, here's the unsubscribe link." [This email reliably gets replies.]

**Newsletter Cadence**

Monthly. First Tuesday of each month. 400–600 words. Structure:
1. One industry observation (what's happening in AU enterprise/government that's relevant)
2. One technical tip (a short, immediately applicable insight)
3. One process/BI resource (blog post, Microsoft announcement, conference worth attending)
4. One opening (if you're currently taking new engagements, say so briefly)

### Measurement Tools

**Google Search Console** (free): Monitors which keywords trigger impressions and clicks. Review weekly. Look for: keywords ranking page 2–3 (positions 11–30) that can be pushed to page 1 with a content update.

**Google Analytics 4** (free): Source/medium breakdown, landing page performance, time on page, scroll depth. Most important: which traffic sources generate contact form submissions.

**LinkedIn Analytics** (native, free): Post reach, impressions, engagement rate by content type. Company page: follower growth by source. Export data monthly.

**Email platform analytics** (Mailchimp free tier, ConvertKit, or Beehiiv): Open rate, click rate, unsubscribes by email in sequence. Target: >35% open rate, >5% click rate on sequence emails.

### Review Cadence

- **Weekly (15 minutes):** Check LinkedIn post performance from the past 7 days; check Search Console for new keyword data; queue next week's posts in PRISM/Buffer
- **Monthly (90 minutes):** Full KPI review against targets; adjust content mix based on what's working; review inquiry pipeline; identify any new keyword opportunities
- **Quarterly (half day):** Channel strategy review; assess whether to start paid search; review conference calendar; update Bid/No Bid pipeline for tender work; assess whether any new services should be promoted
